Green energy for sustainable development in Kenya

  • Nahashon Muiru
  • Jan 1, 2022

Launched by former late President Mwai Kibaki, The Kenyan Government established Kenya Vision 2030 as the nation’s latest growth plan for the time up to 2030. The program aims to revolutionalize Kenya into a newly developed middle-income country that offers a good quality of life for all its citizens. To achieve this, energy will be a vital component as it is crucial in every country’s socioeconomic stability and industrial growth. Dependable and sustainable energy sources are necessary to satisfy human needs including, heating, lighting, safe drinking water, education and communication, among others.

In line with Sustainable Development Goal 7, Kenya aims to achieve universal access to sustainable energy to all, double the global rate of energy efficiency, and double the global share of renewable energy mix by 2030.

Renewable energy has positive contribution by addressing the issue of the climate change and problems related to the environment due to minimal green house gas emissions.

Hydro Electric Power

Accounting for 36% of Kenya's renewable energy mix. Hydropower is Kenya’s single largest electricity generation source with several small hydropower plants being established as stand-alone systems for supplying power to remote areas. Much of the hydroelectric power of Kenya is derived from Tana River. The Seven Forks Hydro Stations are situated along the lower part of the river. The Kenyan government is however strongly pushing for a shift to other alternative sources of electricity generation to be taken so as not to endanger access to clean drinking water when expanding the energy system.

Geothermal Energy

Kenya is the eighth largest geothermal power producer in the world and the largest geothermal producer in Africa. It was one of the first countries in Sub-Sahara Africa to exploit geothermal power on a significant scale.The Geothermal Development Company (GDC), a semi-autonomous government agency, is tasked with developing steam fields and selling geothermal steam for electricity generation to Kenya Electricity Generating Company (KenGen) and Independent Power Producers.

Solar Energy

Kenya is located on the equator and has an excellent potential for utilizing solar energy with an average of 4–6 KWH/M2/day. To achieve the renewable energy goals set forth in “Vision 2030,” the Kenyan government has launched several solar energy projects. The Garissa Solar Power Plant has substantially reduced energy costs in Kenya and has become the largest solar power plant in East and Central Africa. Several other solar power stations that are operational or under construction include: Rumuruti (40 MW), Radiant (40 MW), Eldosol (40 MW), Alten (40 MW), Kenyatta University (10 MW), Malindi (52 MW), and Kopere(50 MW). Companies such as M-Kopa offer pay-as-you-go plans to make solar power products moreaffordable and accessible to low-income households.

Wind Energy

Kenya also has one of Africa’s most abundant and most observed wind power potentials. Results from a national climate evaluation by Kenyan Government Wind Force show that more than 73% of the country has more than 6 m/s of annual mean wind speed at 100 m above ground floor. Recently, Kenya launched the Lake Turkana Wind Power Project, the largest wind power project in Africa and Kenya’s largest public-private investment in history, consisting of 365 turbines with a capacity to generate 310 MW of energy to the national grid..

Conclusion

Even though Kenya has made strides in the use and utilization of renewable energy, its application in generating power is still very minimal. Taking into account the natural environment, coupled with its geographical features Kenya is endowed with many renewable energy sources: geothermal, solar, biomass, wind and hydro. The government is focusing its attention towards harnessing this huge potential. Huge strides have been taken in promoting use of renewable energy such as formulation of policies and regulations for example, National Energy Policy, 2006 Act on Energy and many others. These efforts have not yet yielded substantial results. A well planned and driven effort should be employed by all the stakeholders in public sector, non-governmental organization and the citizens to promote and encourage the use of renewable energy in order to achieve sustainable development.
